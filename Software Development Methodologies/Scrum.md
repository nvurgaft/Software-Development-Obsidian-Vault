Scrum is one of the most popular Agile frameworks, helping teams tackle complex projects by breaking work into smaller, iterative cycles called sprints. It’s designed to boost collaboration, increase transparency, and drive continuous improvement.

Whether you’re building software, managing IT requests, or coordinating cross-functional projects, the Scrum methodology connects meetings, tools, and roles that work in concert to help you and your team structure and manage work.

### Key Takeaways

- Scrum is an [[Agile]] framework that structures work into time-boxed sprints, with defined roles, artifacts, and ceremonies for iterative delivery.
- Key roles include product owner, Scrum master, and development team, all collaborating to achieve sprint goals.
- Scrum emphasizes transparency, inspection, and adaptation, enabling teams to respond to change and deliver value incrementally.
- Start by organizing your team’s work into sprints and adopting Scrum ceremonies to improve focus and project delivery cadence.

Agile is based on four core values:

1. **Individuals and interactions** over processes and tools.
2. **Working software** over comprehensive documentation.
3. **Customer collaboration** over contract negotiation.
4. **Responding to change** over following a plan.

This doesn't mean documentation or planning are unimportant—it means they shouldn't get in the way of delivering value.

## The Framework

Scrum divides development into short iteration called sprints, Sprints last between 1 to 4 weeks (2 weeks sprints are very common).

At the end of each sprint, the development team should have a deployable product.

This adds flexibility to the development cycle, instead of committing to create a set product in 6 months, ship the users new features every couple of weeks.

## Roles

Scrum defined 3 main roles 

### Product Owner

Represents the customer (his interests and need) and is responsible for maximizing the product value for him.

Responsibilities include:

- Prioritizing features - What the client needs now or soon rather then later.
- Managing the Product Backlog
- Clarifying requirements - Makes sure the development team ships what was requested.
- Accepting completed work.
### Scrum Master

The Scrum Master is a **facilitator**, not a traditional project manager.

Responsibilities include:

- Removing blockers
- Coaching the team on Scrum practices
- Protecting the team from interruptions
- Facilitating Scrum events

**Example:**

If developers are blocked because they lack database access, the Scrum Master works to resolve that issue.
### Development Team

Usually a cross-functional, self-managing team that builds and ships the product.

The team typically includes:

- Backend developers
- Frontend developers
- QA engineers
- UX designers
- DevOps engineers

Rather than working in isolated departments, they collaborate to deliver complete features.

## Product Backlog

The Product Backlog is an ordered list of everything that might be built.

Features such as login, registration, forgot password, user details, shopping cart, dashboards are all big features that should be placed in the backlog.

## Spring planning

At the start of the sprint, the teams decides what work they can complete. The Product owner may prioritize some backlog items.

**Example:**

If there are, lets say, 20 backlog items, 5 will be chosen for a sprint. The next sprint may have 3 items because they require move time to develop and test.

## Sprint

The team works on the current sprint without adding new work unless absolutely necessary.

# Daily Scrum (Stand-up)

Every day, the team holds a short meeting (typically 15 minutes).

Each member answers:

- What did I complete yesterday ?
- What will I work on today ?
- Am I blocked by anything or anyone ?

**Example:**

Developer:

> Yesterday I finished the login API.
> 
> Today I'll work on password hashing.
> 
> I'm blocked waiting for the database schema.

The goal is coordination, not status reporting to a manager.
